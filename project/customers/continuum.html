The before.

In 2024, Continuum's medical director Dr. Daria Kowalski was running a longevity practice across four clinics — and five vendor dashboards. Apple Health for one set of patients. Whoop for another. Garmin for the runners. A bespoke spreadsheet for the bloods.

"Every Monday, we spent the first two hours of clinic reconciling data instead of reading it," she remembers. "Patients showed up for their quarterly with a watch, a ring, a scale, and three apps — and we were the part of the system holding all of it together with PDFs."

We retired five dashboards in a weekend. Our clinicians stopped reconciling data and started reading it. Dr. Daria Kowalski · Medical Director, Continuum

The rollout.

Continuum went live on BiotrackOS in a single weekend. On Friday afternoon, their head of operations sent a one-tap consent link to every active patient. By Sunday night, 92% had connected — most without writing back.

"It was the easiest patient communication we've ever sent," Kowalski says. "One screen, one button, no app downloads they hadn't already done."

What changed.

Six weeks in, Kowalski's clinicians had stopped opening every other dashboard. By month two, the Continuum team had built nine custom cohort views — by program, by year of enrollment, by adherence — and was running their weekly clinical review off three saved searches.

"Reconciliation used to be a job. Now it's just a screen."

The bigger surprise was patient-side. Continuum's white-labeled consumer app — same data, on the patient's phone — drove a 41% lift in self-reported adherence within 90 days. "People look at their numbers because they can finally see them all at once," Kowalski says.

Today.

Continuum is rolling BiotrackOS across two more clinics in 2026 and using the API to pipe deduplicated records into their Epic instance — closing the loop they'd been holding shut with spreadsheets for six years.

"It feels like the boring infrastructure we always wanted," Kowalski says. "And our patients tell us their bodies feel less like a mystery. That's the whole job."
